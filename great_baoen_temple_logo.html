Logo Design · Visual Design · Website Identity

Logo for Great Bao'en Temple Website

A visual identity study for a digital heritage platform, connecting architectural memory, Buddhist cultural history, and contemporary interactive storytelling.

Logo Design Visual Design Website
01

Project Context

This group project has been in development since the end of January. Our aim was to create a digital platform centred on a specific architectural site, transforming fragmented and complex information about the building into an experience that is more intuitive, accessible, and easy to understand, while giving the website a visual language suited to contemporary audiences.

We ultimately chose the Great Bao'en Temple in Nanjing. What attracted us was not only the architecture itself, but also its long history of repeated construction, destruction, and reconstruction. Sources relating to the temple are dispersed across historical texts, missionary accounts, archaeological research, and modern digital reconstructions. Existing online content, meanwhile, often focuses on tourism or on the surface image of the "Porcelain Tower," rarely bringing the temple's religious context, architectural evolution, and role in cross-cultural exchange into a single, coherent narrative.

02

Timeline as Structure

This quality of continual transformation across past and present made the Great Bao'en Temple particularly well suited to a web-based presentation. We therefore used a timeline as the website's principal structure. As visitors scroll through the page, the architectural form gradually changes, helping them understand the site's journey from a historical monument and archaeological ruin to its contemporary reconstruction.

03

Logo Design Question

As part of the project, I designed a dedicated logo for the website. The central question behind the design was: how could a simple visual identity express both the historical character and the contemporary relevance of the Great Bao'en Temple?

I used the pagoda as the primary identifying image, retaining recognisable architectural features such as the finial, the tiered body, and the base. On the one hand, the vertically extended composition reflects the solemn, upward-reaching quality traditionally associated with Buddhist pagodas. On the other, I drew particular inspiration from the form of the contemporary structure. Through simplified lines, an open framework, and overlapping layers, the design conveys the transparency and lightness of modern architecture, while creating an effect reminiscent of light refracting across a glazed surface.

04

Bilingual Identity

Below the pagoda, the Chinese name "大报恩寺" is rendered in a calligraphic style, creating a deliberate contrast with the rational, modern geometry of the architectural form above it. The English name, "GREAT BAO'EN TEMPLE," completes the bilingual identity and allows the logo to support the website's intended audience of both Chinese and international visitors. The overall design uses a restrained dark brown palette. This evokes historic architecture, timber construction, and aged documents, while retaining a warmer and more human quality than pure black.

05

Historical Compression

I did not want the logo to function merely as a miniature copy of the building's exterior. Instead, it can be understood as a compression of the Great Bao'en Temple's entire timeline: the traditional pagoda form, the contemporary structure, and the continuing process through which history is revisited and reinterpreted all coexist within a single visual symbol.

Ultimately, the logo became the starting point that connects "historical heritage" with "digital experience" across the website. It also encapsulates the central question that shaped the project as a whole: how can we use the design language of the present to make visible once again a building that has repeatedly disappeared and been rebuilt?
