A Magic Mirror of Face Flora

A webcam-based interactive work where the viewer's face becomes a site for particles, mushrooms, crystals, starlight, floating orbs, and flowing backgrounds generated by code.

Processing Computer Vision Procedural Generation Interaction
Introduction

‘A Magic Mirror of Face Flora’ is a digital interactive creation centred on the theme of ‘Real vs Synthetic’.

The work was inspired by the desire to create a surrealist fantasy world, and was influenced by Lauren Moffatt’s interactive digital piece ‘Chorcorallium’, which was exhibited at the Beta Festival in Dublin Port last year; the singing corals in that piece provided me with a great deal of inspiration.

Another important source of inspiration for the work initially came from medieval and Renaissance grotesque imagery. I was particularly interested in the strange yet compelling method of assembling heterogeneous elements found in the works of Giuseppe Arcimboldo and Hieronymus Bosch, where human, natural, fantastical and symbolic forms often appear to merge into hybrid visual bodies.

In addition, I was also influenced by the rich botanical elements found in late Renaissance Flemish floral still life painting. These references encouraged me to imagine the face not simply as a realistic image, but as a magical site where organic, decorative and artificial forms could gather, grow and transform around human presence.

Initially, I envisioned a larger, more complex system that emphasised the contrast between two worlds: one organic, constantly growing like coral, plants or fungi; the other more artificial, rule-based, expanding like cell division.

However, when it came to the actual implementation, I gradually realised that having an idea and being able to execute it reliably are two entirely different matters.

Consequently, my final work focuses more on a clearly defined interactive relationship: the viewer’s real face is captured via a webcam, and the system generates a complete set of visual responses which, imbued with a sense of fantasy, synthesis and programming, based on this ‘real presence’.

For me, the ‘real’ is most directly embodied in the camera footage and the human face itself; it originates from the real world and possesses a physicality.

The ‘synthetic’, on the other hand, is manifested in the particles, mushrooms, crystals, starlight, floating orbs, flowing background lines and constantly shifting backgrounds generated by code. These are not pre-existing images, but are continuously calculated according to algorithmic rules.

The technical theme I ultimately chose centres primarily on interaction, whilst clearly incorporating procedural generation, as well as elements of computer vision, as I did indeed use OpenCV for face detection.

The user is not simply viewing a pre-made animation, but actually influences the visual composition: when the system detects a face, the decorative elements in the scene begin to orbit around it; when the face moves out of the frame, they return to a state of free-floating.

Although this transition isn’t particularly complex, I believe it sufficiently demonstrates that ‘interaction’ is not merely decorative, but genuinely drives the visual outcome.

Most of the visual content in the project is code-generated, such as particle systems, background gradients, Perlin noise lines, and the movement patterns of mushrooms and light orbs; these are controlled through parameters, random numbers, trigonometric functions and lifecycles.

For instance, particles have their own velocity, decay rate and death time; certain elements enter an orbital state once a face is detected, and these orbits themselves are calculated using cosine and sine functions.

In other words, this piece does not simply display the webcam feed; rather, it uses the live image as a trigger, allowing a synthetic visual layer to continuously grow and emerge.

I feel that the most challenging part of the entire implementation process was the stability of the webcam and face detection. This actually turned out quite differently from what I had initially anticipated.

I had originally thought the hardest part would be ‘how to make the visuals more beautiful’ like creating a fantasy world, but when I actually got down to it, I realised that what held me up first was ‘how to prevent it from breaking’.

Face detection proved particularly problematic. When integrating Java and OpenCV within Processing, both the decision-making logic and real-time performance proved highly sensitive, so I tried numerous versions without much success.

Initially, the system either failed to detect faces, suffered from screen flickering, or encountered bugs. Eventually, I had to continuously pare down and simplify the code, then gradually reintroduce features. I also sought advice from senior students, and only then did the system gradually stabilise.

One of the key issues was flickering. I later realised that the cause wasn’t, as I’d initially thought, the colour or transparency, but rather that the camera feed was only displayed when a new frame was read, whilst the background was refreshed every frame, so the image in between appeared as if it had been ‘wiped away’.

I eventually resolved this by adding a lastFrame variable to cache the previous frame.

Furthermore, to prevent the detection process from becoming too sluggish, I reduced the detection resolution and opted not to perform face detection on every frame, but rather every few frames.

Strictly speaking, these compromises do deviate from the ideal scenario outlined in my original proposal; however, in practice, one often has to make concessions to ensure the system runs, remains stable, and is viable.

If I have more time in the future, what I’d most like to continue working on is deepening the relationship between the ‘face’ and the generated elements. The current system tends to ‘react around the face’, but hasn’t yet delved into the structure of the facial features themselves.

Ideally, I’d like the particles and decorative elements not just to orbit an elliptical outline, but to adhere more closely to finer features such as the eyes, the bridge of the nose and the mouth.

Additionally, I had originally intended to make the ‘synthetic’ aspect more pronounced; for instance, by making it more cellular, fragmented, and resembling the artificial system described in my proposal.

However, due to technical challenges and time constraints, I ultimately prioritised a version that is more stable, complete, and aesthetically cohesive.
